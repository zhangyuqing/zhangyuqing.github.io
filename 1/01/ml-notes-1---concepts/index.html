ML Notes (1) - Concepts

January 1, 1 in Toolbox

Motivation

Machine learning (ML) is by no means new to me. I took ML courses in college and in grad school. In college, I was also in a study group where we went through Bishop’s PRML chapter by chapter. My husband was also learning ML back then, and he did it even more thoroughly - aside from reading, I think he completed at least 70-80% of the exercises in that book. I did some of them too, and at times we would discuss about the problems on our “study dates”. Later when I became a PhD, I used machine learning in many of my projects, from the visualization of data after dimensional reduction through PCA, to building prediction models with logistic regression, random forest, support vector machines, etc. Especially, both my thesis and my intern project with Takeda involve ensemble learning. After all these exposure, I thought ML should feel familar like an old friend by now.

Yet when I talk about it occasionally, it doesn’t feel that way. Instead, I often find myself going back to textbooks or online blogs when I try to explain the rationale behind an algorithm to my friends. I started to realize that, although I am familar with the contents, I have not yet had a chance to build it into a system in my knowledge base. This has motivated me to take a step back and organize my knowledge of ML into a series of notes. And there’s another motivation. Frankly, I have always enjoyed making systematic summaries of the things I learn, even though it takes time. To use the words of Master Shifu (Kung Fu Panda), making these summarises somehow helps me find “inner peace”.

There are literally countless of resources for machine learning theories. My notes are mainly based on the online materials of Stanford CS229. It is one of the best set of materials I’ve seen for someone (like me) with working knowledge of ML to dive deeper into the topics. It also comes with detailed mathematical derivation when necessary, combined with an approachable explanation of the intuition behind math, which I strongly appreciate.

To follow the wise rule of “start simple”, I’ll start my notes from basic concepts.

Concept pairs

  • Supervised / unsupervised learning

Key: labelled or unlabelled data

  • Regression / classification

  • Parametric / non-parametric learning

  • Generative / discriminative models

  • Under-fitting / over-fitting

  • MAP / MLE estimates
